> High-energy demo that built rapport but skipped discovery, left all MEDDPICC elements blank, and closed with a self-serve trial instead of a committed next step — the deal is functionally stuck.

**Style:** Founder-led — Sam's live-account demo and personal backstory created authentic credibility fast, but founder-led energy ran straight into product enthusiasm at the expense of structured discovery — the same authenticity that opened Dana up was never leveraged to slow down and mine her pain, and the call ended with no advance.

## Outcome
- **Intended:** Uncover Crater Labs' product analytics pain, demonstrate Plotline against that pain, identify the buying process and economic buyer, differentiate from Cohortly, and book a concrete next step.
- **Result:** Not achieved
- **Actual:** Sam ran a product-led, feature-tour demo without first discovering Dana's specific pains or metrics. Dana made a passing remark about Cohortly underutilization but it was never explored. Pricing was deflected, no decision process was uncovered, differentiation from Cohortly was never articulated, and the call ended with a vague promise to send a trial login rather than a scheduled follow-up.
- **Advance:** Not secured — No specific, time-bound next step was committed. Sam said 'I'll send you a login, play with it whenever' and Dana responded noncommittally with 'Yeah, maybe.' No date, meeting, or defined action was agreed upon.
- **Missed:** At least one business pain tied to a metric or dollar impact; Demo anchored to pains surfaced in the same call, not a generic tour; Economic buyer and decision process identified; Differentiation vs incumbent or alternative made explicit; Mutual next step scheduled on the call

**Better moves:**
- _Metrics:_ After Dana says 'nobody touches it except one analyst,' ask: 'What does that bottleneck actually cost you — do you know how many product decisions get delayed or made without data each sprint?' Then anchor a number.
- _Economic Buyer:_ Ask Dana directly: 'For a tool that would sit in the product team's stack, who would be involved in the final call — is that you, or do you need your CTO or finance involved above a certain spend threshold?'
- _Decision Criteria:_ Ask: 'If you were to move off Cohortly, what would the new tool have to prove — technically and in terms of adoption — for you to feel confident recommending it internally?'
- _Decision Process:_ Before closing, ask: 'You mentioned a launch in the next few weeks — what does your calendar look like after that, and is that a realistic window to evaluate something like this with the right people in the room?'
- _Paper Process:_ At minimum, plant the flag early: 'Companies your size often have a security review or vendor onboarding process — is that something we should loop in early so it doesn't slow us down later?'
- _Identify Pain:_ When Dana reveals the bottleneck, stop the demo and dig in: 'How often does that slow down a product decision? What happens when a PM needs an answer and the analyst isn't available — do they ship without data or just wait?'
- _Champion:_ Test champion potential by asking: 'If this solved the bottleneck you described, would you be in a position to bring this to the rest of the product team, or would that need to come from someone above you?'
- _Competition:_ Ask: 'What's kept you on Cohortly despite the low adoption — is it contract lock-in, data migration concerns, or something else?' Then tailor the differentiation to those specific switching objections.

## Coaching
**Strengths:**
- Immediate credibility: using his own live Plotline account and sharing the YC/PM backstory created genuine trust and lowered Dana's guard quickly.
- Pain resonance moment: Sam's 'one person becomes the bottleneck' framing visibly connected with Dana's Cohortly situation and surfaced a real, buyer-stated pain.
- Product demonstration was crisp and concrete — the natural-language query live in the call was the strongest value signal of the entire conversation.

**Gaps:**
- Zero discovery before or during the demo: Sam never asked about Dana's goals, current workflow pain, team size, or what success would look like — all MEDDPICC dimensions are empty.
- No advance secured: the call ended with an open-ended trial login and no date, owner, or defined next step, meaning the deal has no momentum.
- Pain surfaced but immediately abandoned: when Dana revealed the Cohortly bottleneck, Sam said 'Right??' and moved on to the next feature rather than probing urgency, consequences, or quantified impact.
- Pricing deflected entirely: Sam's 'we'll figure something out' response to Dana's pricing question missed a qualification and anchoring opportunity.
- Cost of inaction never raised: Sam accepted Dana's 'heads-down on a launch' objection without reframing it as a reason to move faster, leaving no urgency in the deal.

**Top actions:**
1. Book a structured working session before sending the trial login: email Dana today and propose a specific date — 'Rather than an open trial, let me suggest a 45-minute session where we connect your actual event data and answer three real questions your team has. Can we do Thursday May 7th at 2pm ET?' This converts a soft lead into a concrete advance.
2. Run a pain excavation before every demo: prepare five discovery questions for Dana's role (e.g., 'How many PMs on your team make decisions without data today?' 'What's the analyst queue time for an ad-hoc query?') and ask at least three before touching the screen share — the demo only lands if the pain is already quantified.
3. Identify the economic buyer and map the decision process on the working session call: open with 'Dana, assuming this works for you, walk me through how a tool like this gets approved at Crater — who else needs to be involved and what does that process look like?' then ask to include that person in the next meeting.
